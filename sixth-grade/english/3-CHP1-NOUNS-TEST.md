### Section L: Short Answer

**77.** A common noun names any person, place, thing, or idea (general), like "teacher" or "city." A proper noun names a specific person, place, thing, or idea and is capitalized, like "Mr. Johnson" or "San Francisco."

**78.** A concrete noun names something you can see or touch, like "pizza" or "chair." An abstract noun names an idea, quality, or characteristic, like "freedom" or "happiness."

**79.** When a plural noun already ends in -s (like "boys"), you only add an apostrophe — no extra s. Example: "the boys' shoes" means shoes belonging to more than one boy.

**80.** "Bouquet" is the subject of the sentence (one bouquet), and "of flowers" is just a prepositional phrase describing what's in the bouquet. Since the subject is singular, the verb must be singular too ("is," not "are").

### Section L: Short Answer

**86.** The two types of clauses are independent clauses and dependent clauses. An independent clause expresses a complete thought and can stand alone as a sentence, while a dependent clause cannot stand alone and depends on an independent clause to make sense.

**87.** The three types of dependent clauses are adjective clauses, adverb clauses, and noun clauses.

**88.** A misplaced modifier is too far away from the word it is supposed to modify, making the sentence confusing — you fix it by moving the modifier closer to the word it describes. A dangling modifier is not clearly related to any word in the sentence — you fix it by adding a subject to make the meaning clear.

**89.** When a dependent clause comes at the beginning of a sentence, a comma follows it. When a dependent clause comes at the end of a sentence, a comma usually does not set it off. When a dependent clause is in the middle of a sentence, commas are placed on both sides of it.

**90.** Three relative pronouns: who, whom, whose, which, or that (any three). Three subordinating conjunctions: after, although, because, before, if, since, unless, until, when, where, or while (any three).

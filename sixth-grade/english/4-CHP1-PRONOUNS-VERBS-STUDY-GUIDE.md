# Chapter 1: Pronouns & Verbs — Study Guide

Hey! Now that you're a noun expert, let's tackle the two other biggest word types: **pronouns** (words that stand in for nouns) and **verbs** (words that show action or being). Once you master these, you'll be able to read and write WAY more clearly.

---

## Part 1: What Is a Pronoun?

> **A pronoun is a word that takes the place of one or more nouns (or other pronouns).**

Imagine how annoying it would be if we had to keep repeating names:

> *Taylor loves Taylor's cat. Taylor feeds Taylor's cat every morning.* 😴

Pronouns fix that:

> *Taylor loves **her** cat. **She** feeds **it** every morning.* ✨

The word the pronoun replaces is called the **antecedent** (big word, simple idea — it's just "the thing the pronoun is pointing to").

### Examples of Antecedents

- **Jerry** lost **his** hat. → *Jerry* is the antecedent, *his* is the pronoun.
- **Beth** and **I** bought food with **our** money. → *Beth* and *I* are the antecedents, *our* is the pronoun.

---

## Part 2: Personal Pronouns

**Personal pronouns** are the ones you use most. They show two things:
1. **Number** — singular (1) or plural (more than 1)
2. **Person** — 1st (the speaker), 2nd (the one spoken to), 3rd (the one spoken about)

### Personal Pronouns Chart

| | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| **1st Person** (the speaker) | I, me | we, us |
| **2nd Person** (the person addressed) | you | you |
| **3rd Person** (person/thing discussed) | he, him, she, her, it | they, them |

### Examples

- The myth amuses Kim. **It** amuses **her**.
- Raymond and Carl offered the dog a treat. **They** offered **it** a treat.

---

## Part 3: Possessive Pronouns

> **Possessive pronouns show ownership and replace a possessive noun.**

Instead of "Mom's pie is delicious," you can say "**Her** pie is delicious."

### Two Types of Possessive Pronouns

| | Used **Before** a Noun | Used **Alone** |
|---|---|---|
| **Singular** | my, your, his, her, its | mine, yours, his, hers, its |
| **Plural** | our, your, their | ours, yours, theirs |

### Examples

- **Before a noun:** "**My** book is on the table."
- **Alone:** "That book is **mine**."

### ⚠️ BIG WARNING — No Apostrophes!

Possessive pronouns NEVER use apostrophes. They already SHOW possession by themselves.

- ❌ her's / hers'
- ✅ hers
- ❌ it's (this means "it is"!)
- ✅ its (belonging to it)
- ❌ their's
- ✅ theirs

### Memory Trick

**Possessive pronouns = no apostrophe.** The apostrophe belongs to contractions (like "it's" = "it is").

---

## Part 4: Reflexive & Intensive Pronouns

> **Reflexive pronouns refer back to the subject (the antecedent) used earlier in the same sentence.**

They end with **-self** or **-selves**.

### Reflexive Pronouns Chart

| Singular | Plural |
|---|---|
| myself | ourselves |
| yourself | yourselves |
| himself / herself / itself | themselves |

### Examples

- I helped **myself** since no one was home. (*I* is the antecedent)
- It protected **itself**. (*It* is the antecedent)
- We fed **ourselves** for dinner. (*We* is the antecedent)
- You defend **yourselves**. (*You* is the antecedent)

### ⚠️ CAUTION — Fake Pronouns!

These are NOT real words. Don't use them!

| ❌ Wrong | ✅ Right |
|---|---|
| hisself | himself |
| theirselves | themselves |

- ❌ He moved **hisself** into position.
- ✅ He moved **himself** into position.
- ❌ They built **theirselves** a garage.
- ✅ They built **themselves** a garage.

---

## Part 5: Demonstrative Pronouns

> **Demonstrative pronouns point out specific people, places, or things.**

Only 4 of them: **this, that, these, those**.

### The Chart

| | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| **Physically close** | this | these |
| **Far away** | that | those |

### Examples

- **This** is your sister. (close, singular)
- **These** are your gloves. (close, plural)
- **That** is a large dog. (far, singular)
- **Those** are wild geese. (far, plural)

### ⚠️ CAUTION

**"Them" is NOT a demonstrative pronoun.**

- ❌ **Them** are my parents.
- ✅ **Those** are my parents.

**Don't add "here" or "there" after a demonstrative.**

- ❌ **This here** is an expensive guitar.
- ✅ **This** is an expensive guitar.
- ❌ **That there** is a hornet!
- ✅ **That** is a hornet!

### Memory Trick

**THIS/THESE = close enough to touch.** **THAT/THOSE = over there.** If you add an "S", it's plural.

---

## Part 6: Indefinite Pronouns

> **Indefinite pronouns refer to one or more UNSPECIFIED people, places, or things.**

They don't name a specific thing — they're kind of vague on purpose.

### Examples in Sentences

- Does **anyone** know the story of Midas?
- **Each** was out of place.
- **Neither** was selected.
- **Anybody** can play the game.

### Some Indefinite Pronouns

| Singular | Plural |
|---|---|
| another, anybody, anyone, anything | both |
| each, either, everybody, everyone | few |
| everything, much, neither, nobody | many |
| no one, nothing, one, somebody | others |
| someone, something | several |

### Memory Trick

If a word ends in **-body, -one, or -thing** (like every**body**, some**one**, any**thing**), it's almost always an indefinite pronoun AND singular.

---

## Part 7: Interrogative Pronouns

> **An interrogative pronoun introduces a question.**

Only 5: **who, whom, whose, what, which**.

### Examples

- **Who** is Pandora?
- For **whom** does Hephaestus make a staff?
- **What** is Pandora's curiosity about?
- **Which** did you buy?
- **Whose** do you borrow?

### Usage Rules

- **Who/whom** → refer to people
- **What** → refer to things or animals
- **Which** → refer to people or things
- **Whose** → shows possession (people or things)

---

## Part 8: Who vs. Whom (The Classic Trap!)

Both refer to people, but they work differently.

- **Who** is used as a subject (the one doing the action)
- **Whom** is used as an object (receiving the action, or after a preposition like *to, for, with*)

### The Super Easy Trick

**Substitute HE or HIM into the sentence:**
- If **HE** sounds right → use **WHO**
- If **HIM** sounds right → use **WHOM**

> **HE** and **WHO** both end in vowels. **HIM** and **WHOM** both end in **M**.

### Examples

- (Who / Whom) went to the park?
  - **He** went to the park ✅ → so it's **Who**
  - Him went to the park ❌

- You ate lunch with (who / whom)?
  - You ate lunch with he ❌
  - You ate lunch with **him** ✅ → so it's **Whom**

- To (who / whom) are you speaking?
  - ...are you speaking to **him**? ✅ → **Whom**

### Sometimes You Need to Rearrange

- (Who / Whom) have you invited?
  - Have you invited **her** (him)? ✅ → **Whom**
- To (who / whom) did you speak?
  - Did you speak to **him**? ✅ → **Whom**

---

## Part 9: Whose vs. Who's

Easy to mix up, but different jobs!

- **Whose** = shows possession (interrogative pronoun)
- **Who's** = contraction of "**who is**" or "**who has**"

### Examples

- **Whose** jar is it? (Whose = owner)
- **Who's** reading the myth? (Who's = Who is)

### Memory Trick

If you can replace it with "who is" or "who has," use **who's**. Otherwise, use **whose**.

---

## Part 10: What Is a Verb?

A verb is a word that tells what the subject **does** or **is**. Every complete sentence needs one.

There are two main types:
1. **Action verbs** — show what the subject DOES
2. **Linking verbs** — link the subject to a description (state of being)

---

## Part 11: Action Verbs

> **An action verb tells of or shows an action.**

Two flavors:

### Physical Action (you can see it)
- run, jump, play, swim, throw, write, dance, kick

### Mental Action (you can't easily see it — it happens in your head)
- believe, forget, know, think, wonder, remember, understand

### Examples

- Shelby **runs** fast. (physical)
- I **know** the answer. (mental)
- We **ate** pasta. (physical)
- He **remembers** poetry. (mental)

---

## Part 12: Linking Verbs

> **A linking verb expresses the condition or state of being and serves as a link between words.**

It doesn't show action — it connects the subject to a description.

### The Most Common Linking Verbs — Forms of "Be"

**am, is, are, was, were, be, being, been**

### Examples

- I **am** hungry.
- He **is** tall.
- We **are** tired.
- The food **was** delicious.

Notice each verb is a **link** between the words on either side!

### Other Linking Verbs

**appear, seem, look, smell, taste, feel, sound** (and their various forms)

- I **feel** ill.
- The patient **seems** alert.
- Our soup **tastes** bland.
- The birds **appear** calm.

---

## Part 13: Action vs. Linking — The Test

Some verbs like "smell," "taste," "feel," and "look" can be EITHER action or linking! Here's how to tell:

### The "Be" Substitution Test

**Replace the verb with am/is/are/was/were.**
- If the sentence still makes sense → **linking verb**
- If it doesn't make sense → **action verb**

### Examples

- The clothes **smell** clean. → The clothes **are** clean. ✅ makes sense → **linking**
- I **smell** clean clothes. → I **am** clean clothes. ❌ doesn't make sense → **action**

| Linking (state of being) | Action (doing something) |
|---|---|
| The clothes **smell** clean. | I **smell** clean clothes. |
| This path **looks** safe. | Did you **look** at the path? |
| Our soup **tastes** bland. | Matt **tasted** the soup. |
| The water **feels** warm. | Did you **feel** the water? |

---

## Part 14: Main Verbs and Helping Verbs

> **A main verb expresses the action or being in the sentence.** It tells what the subject *does* or *is*.

> **A helping verb works WITH the main verb to help it express its meaning.**

Together, a main verb + helping verb(s) = a **verb phrase**.

### Examples

- Joe **jumps** the fence. (main verb alone)
- Joe **will jump** the fence. (helping + main)
- Joe **is** tired. (main verb alone)
- Joe **should be** tired. (helping + main)

### Common Helping Verbs

| Group | Helping Verbs |
|---|---|
| **Forms of "be"** | am, is, are, was, were, be, being, been |
| **Forms of "have"** | have, has, had |
| **Forms of "do"** | do, does, did |
| **Future/modal** | shall, will, should, would |
| **Ability/permission** | may, might, must, can, could |

### Verb Phrase Examples

- I **was reading** an exciting book!
- She **has been practicing** for two hours.
- Diego **must have been writing** his letter.

Notice how a verb phrase can have 1, 2, or even 3 helping verbs!

---

## Part 15: Interrupted Verb Phrases

Sometimes other words sneak INSIDE a verb phrase. Don't be fooled — those sneaky words are NOT part of the verb!

### Words That Often Interrupt

**not (n't), never, hardly, surely, always**

These are usually **adverbs**, not verbs.

### Examples

- She **has** *not* **eaten** her lunch.
  → Verb phrase: **has eaten**
- Marcus **will** *always* **help** his mother.
  → Verb phrase: **will help**
- I **have** *never* **heard** that song.
  → Verb phrase: **have heard**
- Stacy **did**n't **know** the answer.
  → Verb phrase: **did know**
- The reporter **will** *surely* **tell** the truth.
  → Verb phrase: **will tell**

### Questions Also Interrupt Verb Phrases!

With questions, flip them into a statement to find the verb phrase:

| Question | Statement | Verb Phrase |
|---|---|---|
| Has Charles left for the store? | Charles **has left** for the store. | has left |
| Will you pass the salt? | You **will pass** the salt. | will pass |
| Did Marcia lose her gloves? | Marcia **did lose** her gloves. | did lose |

### Memory Trick

**If you can pull the word out and the verb phrase still works, it's NOT part of the verb.** "Not," "never," "always," etc. can always be removed.

---

## Quick Recap (Say It Out Loud!)

> "Pronouns replace nouns. Personal pronouns show number and person. Possessive pronouns have NO apostrophe. Reflexive pronouns end in -self or -selves. This/these = close; that/those = far. Who = he; Whom = him. Verbs are action OR linking. A verb phrase = helping verb(s) + main verb. And 'not,' 'never,' 'always' are NEVER part of the verb phrase!"

---

## Tips & Tricks for the Test

1. **No apostrophes in possessive pronouns.** *Its* = belongs to it. *It's* = it is.
2. **Who vs. Whom?** Substitute **he/him**. HIM and WHOM both end in M.
3. **Demonstratives:** This/these = close, That/those = far. Them is NOT a demonstrative.
4. **Indefinite pronouns:** If it ends in -body, -one, or -thing, it's singular.
5. **"-Self" pronouns** must match their subject. He → himself. They → themselves. (NOT hisself or theirselves!)
6. **Action or linking verb?** Try replacing with "am/is/are." If it still makes sense, it's linking.
7. **Finding the verb phrase:** Ignore "not," "never," "always," "surely," "hardly."
8. **For questions:** Flip to a statement to find the verb phrase.
9. **Antecedent:** The noun the pronoun is pointing to. Always ask, "What does this pronoun refer to?"
10. **Read carefully.** Some words can be pronouns OR adjectives (like "this"). Check how it's used.

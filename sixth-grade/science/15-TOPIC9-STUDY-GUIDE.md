# Topic 9: Reproduction and Growth (Genetics) -- Study Guide

Hey! This topic is all about how living things make more living things and why you look the way you do. Genetics is basically the science of WHY you have your mom's eyes and your dad's height (or maybe the other way around). Let's get into it.

---

## Lesson 1: Patterns of Reproduction

### Two Ways to Make Offspring

There are two main ways living things reproduce, and they are very different:

| Type | What Happens | Who Does It | Key Feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Asexual reproduction** | ONE parent produces offspring all by itself | Bacteria, some plants, coral, sponges | Offspring are genetically **IDENTICAL** to the parent -- basically clones |
| **Sexual reproduction** | Genetic info from TWO parents combines to create a new organism | Cattle, dogs, cats, pea plants, humans | Offspring are genetically **UNIQUE** -- a brand new mix |

Think of asexual reproduction like a copy machine -- you put in one original and get an exact copy. Sexual reproduction is more like mixing two different paint colors -- you get something totally new every time.

### Traits and Genes

- **Trait:** A characteristic that gets passed genetically from parent to offspring (eye color, hair texture, height). Important: things like your sense of humor or your favorite food are NOT genetic traits -- those are learned!
- **Gene:** A small section of DNA that determines a specific trait. Think of genes like individual recipes in a cookbook -- each one has instructions for making something specific.
- Genes are basically **instructions for your cells**. For example, the eye color gene has instructions for making the pigment that colors your iris.
- Every organism has **2 sets of each gene** -- one from each parent. So you got one "eye color recipe" from Mom and one from Dad.

### Alleles -- Different Versions of the Same Gene

Here is where it gets interesting:

- **Allele:** A different version or type of the same gene.
- Think of it like shoe brands -- "shoes" is the gene, but Nike, Adidas, and Converse are different alleles. Same category, different versions.
- Example: The gene for arm development has two alleles -- allele **A** (working arms) and allele **a** (non-working). Both are versions of the SAME gene, just different "editions."

### Dominant vs. Recessive Alleles

This is one of the trickiest parts of genetics, so read carefully:

| Type | Letter | Rule | How to Remember |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Dominant allele** | Capital letter (A, B, T) | Always expressed if present -- you only need **ONE copy** | The loud friend who always gets heard |
| **Recessive allele** | Lowercase letter (a, b, t) | "Hidden" behind dominant -- you need **TWO copies** for it to show | The quiet friend who only speaks up when the loud one is gone |

**SUPER IMPORTANT:** Dominant does **NOT** mean "most common." A dominant allele could actually be rare in a population. Dominant just means it masks the recessive allele when both are present.

### Eye Color Example (This Will Be on the Test)

Let's say brown eyes (B) are dominant over blue eyes (b):

- **BB** = brown eyes (two dominant alleles)
- **Bb** = brown eyes (one dominant is enough!)
- **bb** = blue eyes (two recessive alleles -- the only way blue shows up)

So two brown-eyed parents who are both **Bb** can absolutely have a blue-eyed child! There is a **25% chance** the child gets **bb** (one "b" from Mom and one "b" from Dad). We will learn how to figure this out with Punnett squares in Lesson 2.

### Mitosis vs. Meiosis

These two words look almost the same, but they do VERY different jobs:

| | **Mitosis** | **Meiosis** |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Growth and repair | Making egg and sperm cells |
| **Result** | 2 **identical** daughter cells | 4 cells with **HALF** the genetic info |
| **DNA amount** | Full set (complete copy) | Half set (one copy of each gene) |
| **Used for** | Growing taller, healing a cut, replacing old cells | Sexual reproduction |
| **Analogy** | Like photocopying a full document -- you get two exact copies | Like splitting a deck of cards in half -- each pile has only half the cards |

Why does meiosis give half? Because when the egg and sperm come together during **fertilization**, the two halves combine to make a full set again. Half from Mom + half from Dad = one complete you!

### Key Vocabulary Recap

- **Inheritance:** The passing of traits from parents to offspring
- **Fertilization:** When egg and sperm combine, restoring the full set of genes

## Lesson 2: Patterns of Inheritance

### Genotype vs. Phenotype

These two words come up constantly, so nail them down:

| Term | What It Means | Examples | How to Remember |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Genotype** | The combination of alleles an organism has (the genetic code) | AA, Aa, aa | Geno = genes. It is the gene combo you carry inside. |
| **Phenotype** | The physical expression -- what you actually SEE | Brown eyes, blue eyes, tall, short | Pheno = physical. It is the feature that physically shows up. |

The connection:
- **AA** or **Aa** --> **dominant** phenotype (e.g., brown eyes)
- **aa** --> **recessive** phenotype (e.g., blue eyes)

### Purebred vs. Hybrid

| Term | Alleles | Example | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Purebred** | Both alleles are the SAME | AA or aa | "Pure" -- both copies match |
| **Hybrid** | Alleles are DIFFERENT | Aa | "Mixed" -- one of each |

Think of purebred like having two matching socks, and hybrid like wearing one striped sock and one polka-dot sock.

### Gregor Mendel -- The Father of Genetics

- Gregor Mendel was a monk who experimented with **pea plants** in the 1800s
- He discovered the basic patterns of **heredity** (how traits pass from parents to offspring)
- He figured out dominant and recessive traits by carefully cross-breeding pea plants and counting the results
- He is called the **"father of genetics"** because his work laid the foundation for everything we know about inheritance

### Punnett Squares

A **Punnett square** is a tool that helps you predict the possible genotypes and phenotypes of offspring. It is basically a little grid that shows all the ways alleles from two parents can combine.

Here is how it works for a cross between two hybrid brown-eyed parents (Bb x Bb):

```
         Mom
       B     b
     +-----+-----+
  B  | BB  | Bb  |
Dad  +-----+-----+
  b  | Bb  | bb  |
     +-----+-----+
```

**Results:**
- **BB** = 1 out of 4 (25%) -- brown eyes (purebred dominant)
- **Bb** = 2 out of 4 (50%) -- brown eyes (hybrid)
- **bb** = 1 out of 4 (25%) -- blue eyes (purebred recessive)

**Phenotype ratio:** 75% brown eyes, 25% blue eyes (3:1 ratio)

### Probability

- **Probability** is the chance of a particular outcome happening
- In genetics, we use probability to predict what traits offspring might have
- A Punnett square gives us the probability of each genotype and phenotype

### Important Notes

- **Pollination** is how pea plants reproduce (pollen from one plant fertilizes another)
- Most traits are influenced by **MULTIPLE genes**, not just one (height, metabolism, skin color are all controlled by many genes working together)
- Inheritance is **random** -- sexual reproduction creates new, unpredictable combinations of alleles every time. That is why siblings can look so different from each other!

## Lesson 3: Plant Structures for Reproduction

### Two Types of Seed-Making Plants

| Type | Reproduces Using | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Gymnosperms** | Cones | Pine trees, spruce, fir |
| **Angiosperms** | Flowers and fruit | Apple trees, roses, sunflowers |

Think of gymnosperms as the old-school method (cones have been around for hundreds of millions of years) and angiosperms as the newer, flashier method (flowers are like nature's advertisements).

### Flower Parts -- Know These!

Flowers are the reproductive organs of angiosperms. Here are the key parts:

| Part | Male or Female? | What It Does |
|---|---|---|
| **Pistil** | Female | Contains the ovary and ovule; receives pollen |
| **Ovary** | Female | Part of the pistil; holds the ovules; becomes the fruit after fertilization |
| **Ovule** | Female | Inside the ovary; becomes the seed after fertilization |
| **Stamen** | Male | The male structure that holds the anther |
| **Anther** | Male | Part of the stamen; produces pollen |

### How Fertilization Works in Flowers

1. **Pollen** is produced by the anther (male part)
2. Pollen lands on the **pistil** (female part) -- this is called pollination
3. **Fertilization** happens when the pollen reaches the ovule
4. The ovule develops into a **seed**
5. The ovary grows and becomes the **fruit** around the seeds

So when you eat an apple, you are eating the ovary of the flower (the fruit), and the seeds inside are the fertilized ovules. Kind of wild when you think about it!

### How Plants Attract Pollinators

Plants cannot walk around to find a mate, so they need help getting their pollen from one flower to another. They attract pollinators (bees, butterflies, hummingbirds) with:
- **Colorful flowers** -- bright petals act like a neon sign saying "come here!"
- **Scent** -- flowers smell good (or sometimes bad) to attract specific pollinators
- **Nectar** -- a sweet liquid reward that pollinators come to drink

### Germination

**Germination** is when a seed begins to grow into a new plant. The seed needs the right conditions (water, warmth, sometimes light) to "wake up" and start sprouting.

## Lesson 4: Animal Behaviors for Reproduction

### Why Behavior Matters

Animals use specific behaviors to increase their chances of survival and reproduction. Some of these behaviors are learned, but many are built-in.

| Term | What It Means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Instinct** | A behavior an animal is born with -- no learning needed | Sea turtles hatching and immediately crawling toward the ocean |
| **Mating systems** | Strategies animals use for finding and selecting mates | Peacocks displaying colorful tail feathers to attract a mate |
| **Pheromone** | A chemical signal released to attract mates | Moths releasing chemicals that can be detected miles away |
| **Migration** | Seasonal movement of animals from one place to another | Birds flying south for the winter |
| **Parental investment** | The care and resources a parent gives to its offspring | Birds feeding their chicks, mammals nursing their young |

Think of pheromones like nature's perfume -- except instead of just smelling nice, they are sending a specific chemical message that says "I am here and ready to mate!"

## Lesson 5: Factors Influencing Growth

### What Controls How Organisms Grow?

Growth is not just about genetics -- your environment matters too!

- **Genetic factors:** Your DNA sets the blueprint (your potential height, body type, etc.)
- **Environmental factors:** Nutrition, sunlight, water, exercise, and other conditions determine how closely you reach that potential

Think of it like baking a cake: the recipe (genes) tells you what the cake COULD be, but the ingredients and oven temperature (environment) determine how it actually turns out.

### Hormones and Growth

- **Hormones** are chemical messengers that regulate growth in both plants and animals
- In humans, growth hormone tells your bones and muscles to grow
- In plants, hormones control which direction stems and roots grow

### Plant Growth -- Auxin and Tropism

| Term | What It Means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Auxin** | A plant hormone that controls the direction of growth | Auxin collects on the shaded side of a stem, making that side grow faster and bending the plant toward light |
| **Tropism** | A plant's growth response to a stimulus (something in the environment) | Growing toward light (phototropism), growing roots downward with gravity (gravitropism), roots growing toward water (hydrotropism) |

Think of auxin like a traffic director for plant cells -- it tells them which direction to grow. And tropism is just the plant's way of reacting to its surroundings, like how you automatically squint when someone shines a bright light in your face.

### Dormancy

**Dormancy** is a period when growth slows down or stops completely, usually because conditions are not great for growing.

- **Plants:** Many trees go dormant in winter -- they drop their leaves and basically "sleep" until spring
- **Animals:** Hibernation is a form of dormancy -- bears slow way down in winter to conserve energy

Think of dormancy like hitting the pause button on a video game. The game is not over -- the organism is just waiting for better conditions to "unpause" and keep going.

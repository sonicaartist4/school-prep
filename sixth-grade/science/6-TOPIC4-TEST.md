### 09 Short Answer (Questions 23-24)

**23.** Three spheres involved in a mudslide:
- **Hydrosphere** -- the rainstorm provides the water that soaks into the ground and triggers the slide.
- **Geosphere** -- the rocks, soil, and hillside are the land material that collapses and slides downhill.
- **Biosphere** -- the trees and other living things on the hillside are destroyed by the mudslide.
- (Bonus: the **atmosphere** is also involved because rain forms in the atmosphere before falling.)

*Award full credit (2 pts) for naming at least three spheres with correct explanations. Award 1 pt for naming spheres without explaining their roles.*

**24.** The positive feedback loop of glacier melting:
1. Glaciers are white and shiny, so they **reflect** sunlight back into space, keeping the area cool.
2. When temperatures rise, some ice begins to **melt**, exposing dark land or ocean water underneath.
3. Dark surfaces **absorb more heat** from the sun instead of reflecting it.
4. The extra absorbed heat causes **even more ice to melt**, exposing even more dark surface.
5. This cycle keeps repeating and **speeding up** -- more melting leads to more absorption, which leads to more melting.

*Award full credit (2 pts) for describing at least three steps clearly and explaining how the process speeds itself up. Award 1 pt for a partial explanation.*

### 10 Short Answer / Essay (Question 25)

**25.** Sample full-credit response:

The water cycle is the continuous movement of water through Earth's systems. During **evaporation**, liquid water on Earth's surface heats up and turns into water vapor that rises into the atmosphere. Through **condensation**, that water vapor cools down high in the sky and forms tiny water droplets that make up clouds. When the droplets get heavy enough, **precipitation** occurs -- water falls back to Earth as rain, snow, sleet, or hail. **Transpiration** is when water evaporates from the leaves of plants into the air. Transpiration is important because plants release a large amount of water vapor into the atmosphere, making them a major part of the water cycle.

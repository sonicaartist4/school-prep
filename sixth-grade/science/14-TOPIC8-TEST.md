### Section H: Short Answer / Essay

**21.** Sample answer (5 points — 1 point for naming each activity, 1 point for explaining how each adds greenhouse gases, 1 point for overall clarity):

Human activities contribute to climate change in several major ways. First, **burning fossil fuels** (coal, oil, and natural gas) for energy in cars, power plants, and factories releases huge amounts of carbon dioxide (CO2) into the atmosphere -- about 91% of all human greenhouse gas emissions come from this. Second, **agriculture** contributes because livestock like cows produce methane, a powerful greenhouse gas, through their digestive processes. Third, **deforestation** (cutting down forests) makes the problem worse because trees normally absorb CO2 from the air, so when we remove them, there are fewer trees to clean up the CO2 we are producing. Additionally, **industry** and factory operations release CO2 and other greenhouse gases during manufacturing. All of these extra greenhouse gases trap more heat in the atmosphere, causing global temperatures to rise.

**Scoring:** Award 1 point for each correctly named human activity (up to 3 points). Award 1 point for each correct explanation of how that activity adds greenhouse gases (up to 3 points, but cap the total for this sub-part so full question = 5 points). Deduct if the student names natural causes (volcanoes, solar changes) as human activities.

**22.** Sample answer (5 points — 2 points for each effect with explanation, 1 point for overall clarity):

Rising global temperatures have serious effects on polar regions. First, **ice caps and glaciers are melting** at faster rates. As this ice melts, it adds water to the oceans, which causes **sea levels to rise**. Rising sea levels threaten coastal cities and low-lying areas around the world with flooding. Second, **polar ecosystems are being destroyed**. Animals like polar bears depend on sea ice to hunt for food, and as the ice disappears, they lose their habitat and food sources, putting their survival at risk. Penguins and other Arctic and Antarctic species face similar challenges. These effects matter because they do not just stay in the polar regions -- melting ice affects sea levels globally, and losing species disrupts food chains that connect to ecosystems everywhere.

**Scoring:** Award 2 points for each well-explained effect (up to 4 points for two effects). Award 1 point for explaining why these effects matter. Partial credit (1 point) for an effect that is named but not explained.
